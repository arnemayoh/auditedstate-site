Authorship

AI-assisted collaboration introduces layered authorship. Initiation, refinement, validation, and execution may involve multiple agents across time. Preserving authorship requires visible lineage rather than exclusive ownership.

Beyond Single-Origin Models

Traditional authorship assumes a primary originator. AI systems complicate this assumption by contributing analysis, phrasing, and structural suggestions.

However, contribution is not equivalent to responsibility. AI systems extend patterns. They do not assume consequence.

Layered Participation

In AI-assisted environments, authorship may include:

  • The initiator of a question or intention
  • The individual who evaluates and selects outputs
  • The person who validates assumptions
  • The agent who executes or publishes the result

These roles may overlap. They may also be distributed.

What matters is not exclusivity, but visibility.

Prior Art and Revision

Meaningful authorship depends on traceable revision. When earlier reasoning remains visible, contribution becomes contextual rather than ambiguous.

Preserving prior states allows observers to understand how an idea evolved, what was retained, and what changed.

Erasure weakens authorship. Lineage strengthens it.

Delegation and Responsibility

Delegating analysis to AI does not transfer accountability. Responsibility remains with human agents who promote decisions into durable form.

Authorship therefore requires a promotion boundary. Before publication or execution, ownership must be explicit.

Freedom and Recognition

Creative collaboration with AI expands expressive possibility. Structural anchoring does not diminish this expansion. It preserves recognition and responsibility.

Authorship becomes durable when it is traceable.

Relationship to Promotion

Authorship becomes consequential at the moment of Promotion. Exploration may be fluid. Durable decision requires declared ownership.
